What to show the Authorised Person: a calm guide to NSW home-visit evidence

By Adam — founder of Liber and homeschooling parent in NSW · Published 2 July 2026 · Reviewed against current NSW guidance

The short answer: the Authorised Person reviews your records and educational program, not your child's performance. What matters is showing a working system: how you plan, how you record learning as it happens, and how you can see progress across the key learning areas. Everyday learning counts — if you can point to it.

If your first NSW home visit is coming up, you're probably somewhere between "we'll be fine" and quietly rehearsing answers in the shower. I've been there. This guide is what I wish someone had handed me: what the visit actually involves, what "records" really means, and how to walk in calm because the evidence already exists.

One housekeeping note before anything else: home schooling regulation in NSW moved from NESA to the NSW Department of Education in May 2025, and the registration guidelines are being progressively updated. Some documents still say NESA. The requirements below reflect the current published guidelines — always check the live version close to your visit date.

What the Authorised Person actually reviews

The registration requirements come down to five things. In plain English, you need to show:

  1. A program based on the NESA syllabuses — not a packaged curriculum, but a picture of how your child's learning connects to the key learning areas.
  2. That it suits your child — how the program caters for your child's actual learning needs, interests and pace.
  3. A system for planning, supervising and recording — this is the heart of it. Not a perfect system. A real one, that you actually use.
  4. A suitable home learning environment — where learning happens (spoiler: your kitchen table, garden and local creek all count).
  5. Adequate resources — books, materials, library membership, the internet, the world. Adequate, not extravagant.

During the visit, the AP will discuss and review your program with you, and will need to sight your child — but they are not there to test your child. The conversation is with you, about your records.

What "records" can actually look like

There's no prescribed format. Genuinely. Families pass reviews with:

  • A dated journal or diary of learning experiences — a few lines per entry is plenty
  • A photo record with short notes about what the child was working out
  • A folder of work samples collected over time (not assembled in a panic the week before)
  • A blog or digital record organised by date or learning area
  • An app built for this — full disclosure, that's what I built Liber for, so weigh my opinion accordingly. But any of the above can work

What all the workable versions share: entries are dated, they capture what actually happened, and there's some way to see learning across areas and over time. The format matters far less than the habit.

The reframe that changes the visit: everyday learning counts

The critical variable isn't which curriculum you bought — it's whether you can point to the learning in your ordinary days. The afternoon your kid rebuilt a bike derailleur is mechanics, problem-solving and persistence. The argument about whether the umpire was fair is ethics and reasoning. Baking doubled-recipe biscuits is ratio and measurement.

You don't need to dress these up in school language. You need a dated note that it happened and a sentence about what was really going on. An AP who sees a run of those — across months, touching different learning areas — is looking at exactly what the guidelines call "a system for recording teaching and learning experiences."

The week-before checklist

  1. Re-read the current guidelines — they're being updated post-transition; check the live pages, not a Facebook screenshot.
  2. Gather your records into one place — physical folder or one app/screen, so nothing depends on hunting through a camera roll mid-conversation.
  3. Pick 5–8 moments that show range — different learning areas, different months, at least one that shows progress over time.
  4. Write one paragraph on how you plan — even if planning means "we follow interests and I steer with strewing and library trips." Name your system honestly.
  5. Set up a spot for the conversation — a table, your records, a cuppa. The AP is a person; most are former educators who've seen every style of home ed.
  6. Prepare your own questions — about registration periods, reporting, or anything you're unsure of. It's a two-way conversation.

Common questions

Does the Authorised Person test my child?

No. The AP reviews your program and records with you, and needs to sight your child — but the visit is not an assessment of your child's performance.

Do I need a formal curriculum?

Your program must be based on the NESA syllabuses — that's a mapping exercise, not a purchase. Many registered families show how everyday learning connects to syllabus areas instead of using packaged curriculum.

How many work samples do I need?

No fixed number. A modest, consistent, dated record showing a working system beats a thick folder assembled the week before.

Can unschoolers satisfy the requirements?

Yes. The task is translation — showing where syllabus-relevant learning already happens in everyday life, with dated records to point to. It's work, but it's honest work, and it doesn't require changing how your family learns.
